Hi! We are CANDOR!

Our project, Tweet Analysis on False Coronavirus Home Remedies, aims to utilize data science to understand the public perception and beliefs of Philippine Citizens in regards to supposed methods to prevent or cure the SARS-CoV-2 virus. Using data obtained from Twitter and tools such as Python and its libraries, our goal is to provide an analysis that can give insight to the spread of covid-related misinformation.

Here's an overview of our project.

Misinformation has always been a huge problem when it comes to social media, particularly because people tend to accept what they read for what it is, without choosing to check if the information shared is correct. Because of the panic during the early months of the coronavirus pandemic, many people have been desperate for remedies and prevention techniques that they can do from home without spending excessive amounts of money. This eventually lead to them sharing the rumored techniques, secrets and discoveries that can supposedly help which further led to mass sharing of such misinformation.

We chose to analyze this topic specifically because the pandemic has impacted all of our lives, changing it drastically. It further brought to light how many people can be easily deceived or given false hope via misinformation. Through this project, our goal is to find out when was the spread of fake home remedies and techniques more likely to happen and what factors are involved.

Research Question: When did tweets about Covid home remedies/prevention techniques become more likely to go viral in relation to the Covid-19 pandemic in the Philippines?

Hypothesis

Tweets about Covid home remedies/preventions are most likely to go viral during the first months of the pandemic in the country and less likely as time goes on.

Null Hypothesis

Tweets about Covid home remedies/preventions are likely to go viral anytime during the pandemic (first case to 2022).

Action Plan

Analyze the engagements and/or frequency of tweets about Covid home remedies or preventions and correlate it with the date it was posted in relation to the beginning of the pandemic in the country (eg. post date - date of first case).

Here's what we found out.

From exploring the data, we discovered that there were more anonymous accounts than media or identified. This has to do with the way Twitter functions. Since it is not required to identify yourself on the platform, username and unrelated display names are far more common, making it difficult to identify the individuals behind the accounts. Furthermore, it is observed that there are far more misinformation tweets in the early months of the pandemic, before slowing down and experiencing small spikes after.

From our statistical test and machine learning model, we found that date has a linear relationship with engagement and tweet count. We also found that there were four distinct peaks over the course of the pandemic where there were more misinformation tweets than average. More peaks occurred in 2021, but the highest peak occurred on March 2020.

To reiterate the results from our event detection model, the highest peak occurred on March 2020, when President Rodrigo Duterte first places the Philippines under lockdown due to the first local transmission of the coronavirus. At this moment in time, panic and anxiety worsened. With no vaccines or cures in sight, it led many people to speculate and search for supposed home remedies that could prevent or cure COVID-19. The next peak occurred on March 2021, a year after the first peak. However, there are significantly less tweets due to having more time for known health authorities to spread more information about the virus and what to do. This was also the time that the country hit one million cases and also when vaccines started to roll in. However, there was still a noticeable rise in tweets since many people may doubt the vaccines' effectiveness, especially for the vaccine-averse or from misinformation being spread by anti-vaxxers. The next peak occurs on August 2021, when the Delta variant becomes the dominant strain of the virus in the Philippines. Increasing unrest and worry resulted in more tweets. The last peak occurs on January 2022, with the Omicron variant spreading and a medicine shortage occurring. With medicine being out of reach for the time being, many turn to home remedies.

These findings emphasize that misinformation spread during the earlier years of the pandemic, decreasing in engagement as time passed due to misinformation countermeasures. Furthermore, the significant relationship between the date and engagement and tweet count allows us to reject our null hypothesis.

For future steps in the event this study is expanded upon or continued, our recommendations would be to look into more specific events causing the misinformation and to collect more samples in order to identify other minor events and improve the research. Additionally, the virality of certain keywords in relation to false home remedies can be looked into more.
